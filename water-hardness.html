Why Marlow Has Very Hard Water

Marlow sits on a geological area rich in chalk and limestone. As rainwater filters through these layers, it naturally absorbs minerals such as calcium and magnesium. These minerals remain dissolved in the water as it travels through the supply network, resulting in what is known as “hard water”.

This is typical across much of Buckinghamshire and the Thames Valley region.

What Hard Water Means

Hard water is safe to drink and use, but it behaves differently from soft water. When heated or left to evaporate, the dissolved minerals form solid deposits known as limescale. These deposits can build up on surfaces, inside appliances, and within pipework over time.

Where Limescale Commonly Appears

  • On taps, shower heads and metal fittings
  • Inside kettles, boilers and hot water cylinders
  • On tiles, screens and surfaces where water dries
  • Inside pipework, especially older or narrower sections

How Hard Water Affects Plumbing Systems

Reduced flow over time

Limescale can gradually narrow internal pipe diameters, reducing flow rates and affecting pressure at outlets.

Appliance efficiency

Heating elements in kettles, boilers and washing machines can accumulate scale, which may reduce efficiency over time.

Appearance of fittings

White or chalky marks may appear on taps, shower screens and tiles where water evaporates.

Temperature‑related build‑up

Limescale forms more quickly in hot water systems, so showers, boilers and kettles tend to show the most visible effects.

Typical Patterns Seen in Marlow

  • Frequent limescale on taps and shower heads
  • White marks on glass screens and tiles
  • Reduced flow in older pipework or fittings
  • More noticeable build‑up in hot water outlets
